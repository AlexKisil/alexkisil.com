DREAMWILLOW Postmortem

From Pitch to Completion

As WolverineSoft announced the club would be trying its hand at making games, I prepared a pitch for a 2D top-down dungeon-crawler that would become the studio's first semester-long project, DREAMWILLOW. Here, I reflect on what went right, what could've gone better, and what I think were the most valuable lessons learned throughout the process.

Before reading the postmortem, you can check out general information about the game and my responsibilities on it here.

What Went Right

  • Pod system established constant communication between all people working on the same task
  • Offering school credit made for more reliable commitments from members
  • The modularity of the game made it easy for features to be cut when necessary without breaking the game
  • Actively updated public tasks/schedule allowed for transparency of who was working on what and how we were doing as a team
  • Events-based style of programming enabled abstract interfaces between code, audio, and animation
  • Biweekly group playtests gave us a constant stream of feedback on the game
  • The game was finished and showcased at the UM+EMU games showcase to positive reception!

What Could've Gone Better

  • Didn't switch to the pod system until several weeks into the development cycle, causing a breif period of confusion during the transition
  • Documentation became decentralized and outdated as the game grew
  • Didn't enforce a content-lock until very late in development, causing a debugging crunch
  • Many of our processes evolved along the way, meaning team members had to take time mid-development to learn new skills
  • Very little time for pre-production made it difficult to hit the ground running
  • Didn't dedicate enough time to code reviews
  • Unclear version control protocol led to git conflicts on scenes and prefabs

Lessons Learned and Final Thoughts

I pitched the concept for DREAMWILLOW back in September, hoping the simplicity of the game would allow for the studio to get a finished game under its belt. Since then, the game morphed into a culmination of many brainstorming sessions, sleepless nights, rabit holes, Enter the Gungeon breaks, and playtests. The awesome effort of everyone on the team is blinding in the finished product, and because of that I couldn't be more proud. Being able to work alongside and learn from the amazing designers, programmers, artists, audio engineers, and producers on the team was an absolute honor and pleasure. If any of you from the WolverineSoft Studio happen to be reading this: truly from the bottom of my heart, thank you.

While I'm certain that everyone on the team is proud of DREAMWILLOW, I can't help but look forward with excitement as we begin to apply all of the lessons we learned these past few months:

  • Decide pods and tasks early on so that everyone feels like they're contributing
  • Use scriptable objects for an even MORE general interface for designers
  • Keep documentation as centralized as possible
  • Playtest more often with people outside the studio
  • Having a better understanding of audio and art software can allow for more meaningful discussion between designers and the team members that use them
  • The clearer we are with tasks and documentation, the quicker team members can finish assigned work, and the more the morale of the team increases
  • Too many more to fit on this webpage

If you've read this far, thanks so much! This project was really special to me, and I hope that you were able to learn something too, or at least simply enjoy the read.
