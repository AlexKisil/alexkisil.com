Infectant Postmortem

The Proof is in the Inflammation

As the club winded down for summer vacation, some passionate members of WolverineSoft decided to see if it was possible to create a finished, compelling game over the course of 9 weeks with relatively little experience. Here, I reflect on what went right, what could've gone better, and what I think were the most valuable lessons learned throughout the process.

Before reading the postmortem, you can check out general information about the game and my responsibilities on it here.

What Went Right

  • The simplicity of the player controls let us focus on making the game feel good rather than adding complex progression systems
  • Actively updated public tasks/schedule allowed for transparency of who was working on what and how we were doing as a team
  • First time many members got to experience working in multi-disciplinary teams
  • The game was completed -- a first for many members of the team
  • Awesome art previews were frequently posted for the team to see, boosting the team's morale

What Could've Gone Better

  • The development cycle taking place in the summer meant many team members were working remotely and couldn't meet
  • Communication from leaders broke down near the end of development, meaning tasks were no longer being completed or reviewed
  • Never enforced a content-lock, causing the finished product to have many bugs that went unnoticed during development
  • A lack of understanding between teams and led to mismatched interfaces and lost work
  • Very little time for pre-production made it difficult to hit the ground running

Lessons Learned and Final Thoughts

Infectant essentially served as a test-run for WolverineSoft's new studio component at the University of Michigan. The game was made by a group of passionate students and graduates that didn't necessarily have any experience, but wanted to make a game. Because of this, we faced a lot of new challenges and grew together as a team. Unfortunately, Infectant is a game that is probably weaker than the sum of its parts, but the lessons I learned while working on the on the project have proven to be invaluable. Most importantly, I met some incredibly talented people that I continue to have the pleasure of making games with today.

Some of the lessons we learned from Infectant:

  • Incremental progress and frequent check-ins should be encouraged
  • System documentation is just as important as the code (if not more important!)
  • Content locks are important to leave time for debugging existing features
  • Playtesting should be prioritized to avoid pursuing features that aren't resonating with the players
  • Frequent discussion between teams can lead to a quicker, cleaner workflow
  • Git does not play nice with Unity Scenes and Prefabs

If you've read this far, thanks so much! This project was a massive learning experience for me, and I hope that you were able to learn something too, or at least simply enjoy the read.
